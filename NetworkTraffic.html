Network Traffic Overview

All internet traffic has a source and destination, and Internet Protocol (IP) is how computers keep track of senders and receivers. Every device needs a unique address in order to receive packets (data at the network level). However because IPv4 address are limited, often whole networks share 1 IP address that the outside world can communicate with (your global IP address). Within these networks each computer still has a unique address (your local IP address). To communicate to the outside world, your router (which is also your default gatewayâ€™s IP address) uses Network Address Translation (NAT). Nat Overload keeps track of connections via port, and hides your local computer from the outside world. If someone wants to start a connection to your machine, your router must allow it through port-forwarding. Port-forwarding takes incoming traffic, and sends it to your local machine based on the destination port. Potentially, someone can exploit this to get onto your network, so it is recommended you only leave port-forwarding on when you are using it.
